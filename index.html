Important Historical Events

Babylon

The oldest written mathematical algorithms date back nearly 3800 years ago. Several translated Babylonian clay tablets reveal that the Babylonians developed general methods for solving classes of problems.

The Origins

Near the end of the fourteenth century, Ibn al-Majdi, an Egyptian astronomer, wrote a text comparing two algorithms: the method of translation and the method of semi-translation. This is the first documented case of the analysis of algorithmic complexity.

The Entscheidungsproblem

In 1936, both Alonzo Church and Alan Turing independently published papers proving that the Entscheidungsproblem had no answer. Their work would form the basis of theoretical computer science.

A Perplexing Observation

In 1965, Juris Hartmanis and Richard Edwin Stearn wrote a paper titled "On the Computational Complexity of Algorithms," tying together the work of Church and Turing, and developing the notion of algorthmic complexity.

Algorthmic Complexity Examples

Merge Sort

complexity: O(nlog(n))

Merge sort is a divide and conquer algorithm that was invented by John von Neumann in 1945. Merge sort works by dividing a list into n sublists then reepeatedly merging those sublists until the list is sorted.

Insertion Sort

complexity: O(n^2)

Insertion sort is a simple sorting algorithm that builds the final sorted array (or list) one item at a time. It does not perform well on large inputs.

Seleciton Sort

complexity: O(n^2)

selection sort is an in-place comparison sorting algorithm. The algorithm divides the input list into two parts: a sorted sublist of items which is built up from left to right at the front (left) of the list and a sublist of the remaining unsorted items that occupy the rest of the list.

Bubble Sort

complexity: O(n^2)

Bubble sort, sometimes referred to as sinking sort, is a simple sorting algorithm that repeatedly steps through the list, compares adjacent elements and swaps them if they are in the wrong order.

Bogosort

complexity: O((n+1)!)

Bogosort is a highly inefficient sorting algorithm based on the generate and test paradigm. The function successively generates permutations of its input until it finds one that is sorted.

Famous Names

Donald Ervin Knuth is an American computer scientist, mathematician, and professor emeritus at Stanford University. He is the 1974 recipient of the ACM Turing Award, informally considered the Nobel Prize of computer science. Knuth has been called the "father of the analysis of algorithms".

Alonzo Church was an American mathematician and logician who made major contributions to mathematical logic and the foundations of theoretical computer science.

Alan Mathison Turing OBE FRS was an English mathematician, computer scientist, logician, cryptanalyst, philosopher, and theoretical biologist. He made major contributions to the foundations of theoretical computer science as well.
